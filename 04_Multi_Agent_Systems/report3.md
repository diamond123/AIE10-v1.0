## Overview
The verified sources support a practical, prevention-first approach to obesity in adult indoor cats: measured feeding, avoiding food-as-reward, and feeding methods that simulate hunting or foraging can help reduce overeating and support weight management [1]. The guidance also emphasizes enrichment and feeding structure, particularly in multi-cat households where competition, food stealing, and boredom can contribute to excess intake [2].

## Strong conclusions supported by the verified sources
1. **Measured food portions are preferred over unstructured feeding.** The FelineVMA behavior guideline states that the amount of food should be calculated and measured out to prevent overeating [1].
2. **Food should not be the main way owners show affection.** The same guideline recommends giving cats positive attention rather than food rewards as a prevention strategy [1].
3. **Feeding methods that mimic hunting/foraging can be helpful.** The guideline notes that feeding methods should simulate normal feeding behavior, including ways to allow cats to hunt for their food, which can increase exercise during feeding time and decrease boredom [1].
4. **Enrichment matters, especially in multi-cat homes.** FelineVMA guidance notes that enrichment can help prevent food guarding and bullying and may reduce obesity-related food stealing; protected feeding setups can be useful in obese cats [2].
5. **Routine nutritional assessment should include BCS and MCS, not body weight alone.** The FelineVMA nutrition protocol identifies body weight, body condition score, and muscle condition score as part of nutritional assessment [3].
6. **Safety monitoring is essential.** The same protocol highlights assisted-feeding triggers that include hepatic lipidosis, anorexia of 3 days or more, involuntary weight loss, and decreased muscle condition [3]. This supports the need for close veterinary oversight when intake is reduced or weight is changing.

## Tentative or incomplete evidence
- The verified material in this run does **not** provide fully retrievable comparative evidence on the accuracy or reproducibility of body condition scoring versus morphometric methods, nor on owner-versus-clinician assessment performance.
- The verified material also does **not** provide detailed, citable outpatient protocols for weight-loss rates, monitoring intervals, or specific diet-composition strategies for treatment.
- Prevention guidance is better supported here than treatment guidance; the distinction matters because interventions suitable for prevention are not automatically safe or adequate for active weight reduction.

## Practical interpretation for clinical use
For adult indoor cats, the verified guidance supports focusing on: structured portion control, avoiding food-based affection, using feeding enrichment that encourages activity, and reducing boredom and food competition in shared households [1][2]. For assessment and follow-up, body weight should be interpreted alongside BCS and MCS [3].

Because weight-management can create risk if intake becomes too low, **any feline weight-loss plan should be supervised by a veterinarian**. **Overly rapid weight loss can be dangerous**, and inadequate intake may increase the risk of malnutrition, hepatic lipidosis, and deterioration of concurrent illness [3].

## Meaningful disagreement or limitations in the evidence
There is not much disagreement within the verified sources themselves; the main issue is **incompleteness**. The accessible FelineVMA materials align on broad prevention and safety themes, but they do not fully answer comparative questions about the best assessment method or the safest/effective outpatient treatment protocol. The evidence base retrieved here is therefore strongest for general preventive principles and monitoring priorities, and weaker for precise treatment optimization.
